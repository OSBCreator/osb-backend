The Proxy Caller Exploit — You Are Not the Target. You Are the Mechanism.
You called the bank yourself. Real number. Right questions. They confirmed the balance. You hung up feeling certain. That certainty was the goal. He could not make that call. Not with his accent. Not without leaving a trace. So he spent time love bombing someone who would make it for him — willingly, carefully, proudly. When you confirmed that balance, you did not make a mistake. You completed a step in a criminal operation you were oblivious of being a part of. He wanted more than your money. He wanted your legitimacy. Your accent. The Facade Bank Page — A Staged Interface Designed to Manufacture Certainty
It did not start with asking. It started with showing. A large balance — right there on the screen. He walked you through it calmly, the kind of calm that comes from someone who appears to have nothing to hide. Working. Building something. A man with substance, with means, with a future that included you. You were not being asked to give anything. You were being asked to help someone you trusted move money that was already his — from his bank account. It felt natural. It felt safe. Then something went wrong. A security flag. An account restriction. Hence the monetary request. But the money existed. You had seen it. That feeling of safety was not an accident. It was the architecture of the moment. Because the bank page itself was never real — a staged interface designed to convince the victim that the money already existed. Clarity is not defeat. It is the first step to freedom.

I Know He Is a Scammer. I Still Cannot Let Go.
He did not win your heart in a day. It took time. It took process. So there is no reason your feelings should disappear the moment you find out the truth. The truth changes what it was. It does not change what it felt like. That is the trap inside the trap. The scam continues in your nervous system long after the conversation ends. You do not block him on day one. You reduce. Gradually. Confronting him will not give you closure — it will give him another opening. Erase the digital presence. Every photo. Every saved message. Fill the space. That void is proof you are capable of deep connection. That capacity belongs to you. It was never his. You do not have to have lost money for this to count as damage. Emotional injury is injury.

3 Ways to Spot a Romance Scammer — Before It Is Too Late
Asking for money, bank info, driver licence, or SSN? SCAMMER. Sends you a package? It might seem like a sweet gesture, but scammers use this method to earn your trust and create obligation. Introduced you to cryptocurrency investment? Crypto is used in romance scams due to its anonymity and the impossibility of recovery. "But I video called him?" These are the days of sophisticated AI. Video calls can now be faked using deepfake software. Be vigilant.

The Hidden Dangers of Romance Scams — It Goes Deeper Than Money
EMOTIONAL DAMAGE: Heartbreak and betrayal. Victims form real emotional bonds. Depression, anxiety, even PTSD are common. Many blame themselves, leading to withdrawal or suicidal thoughts. FINANCIAL LOSS: Victims have lost thousands to millions. Some drain life savings, retirement funds, or business capital. Others take loans or remortgage homes under pressure. MIND CONTROL: Scammers use love bombing, fake emergencies, and guilt as weapons. Victims are isolated and taught to distrust family. In extreme cases they unknowingly become money mules. IDENTITY THEFT: Scammers collect photos, IDs, bank info, passwords. Some victims are blackmailed with private images — sextortion. LEGAL TROUBLE: Victims may unknowingly help move stolen money. Some face investigations, frozen accounts, or criminal charges. Shame on anyone who judges victims. Compassion is what is needed — not condemnation.

Oil Rig Scams: The Romance Trap You Did Not See Coming
You meet someone online — charming, professional, claims to be an engineer on an offshore oil rig. They say they are stationed in a remote location and cannot come home yet. After love bombing, they tell you there is a crisis: "My equipment broke down, I need to transfer money urgently, but I cannot access my account from here." They give you a fake bank login and ask you to transfer money. Suddenly the account gets frozen. Then they beg you to cover the cost — "I will pay you back when I return." There is NO oil rig. NO broken equipment. NO real bank account. Just an emotional setup to steal your money.
Scammers use emotions as weapons. Trust — but VERIFY.

Field Case
From Love to Loss: A Singapore Nurse's Story
A hardworking nurse in Singapore. After long shifts and isolation during COVID, she began chatting with a kind man online. He said he was an interior designer in Canada. They talked daily — sweet messages, photos, even dreams of marriage. Then he introduced her to crypto trading. She tried it. The website looked real. Her money grew. Over time she invested all her savings, took a loan, and sent over S$120,000 ($90,000 USD). Then it collapsed. The site demanded withdrawal fees. When she refused, he vanished. Everything — the romance, the money, the platform — was fake. She was left with no money, huge debt, shame, heartbreak, and depression. She later filed for bankruptcy and started therapy.
If this could happen to a smart, strong nurse — it can happen to anyone.

Field Case
The "UN Doctor" Script — $50,000 Paid in Clearance Fees That Never Existed
In one documented case, a victim wired over $50,000 with each payment tied to a new UN processing fee, emergency, or clearance requirement. The payments were spaced out. Each request sounded reasonable. Each crisis felt urgent. That is how this script works. The scammer claims to be a surgeon or humanitarian working for the United Nations. Widowed or divorced. Moves fast emotionally. Introduces a child for sympathy. Creates a crisis — blocked bank account, urgent travel approval. Asks you to pay UN processing or clearance fees. Reality: The United Nations does not charge the public for staff leave, travel, or admin processing. Official emails come only from @un.org domains.
If money enters the relationship, it is not love. It is a scam.

Why People Still Fall Victim in 2026 — Loneliness Is the Real Exploit
Loneliness and lack of awareness are the real roots of this problem. Who rejects love and affection in moments of emotional vulnerability? When attention comes when you are at your lowest, it is difficult to resist. Romance scammers penetrate during grief, loneliness, or major life changes — exploiting emotional vulnerability. Seniors remain among the most vulnerable. The most recent FTC data shows adults aged 60 and above lost approximately $277 million to romance scams alone. When people feel loved and remembered by family, they are less likely to seek affection from strangers online. Human connection remains the strongest defence of all.
FTC Data: Adults 60+ lost ~$277M to romance scams alone.

Warning
Recovery Scams: Do Not Pay Twice
In 2025, the FTC reported that imposter and advance-fee scams — including recovery scams — accounted for nearly $3 billion in reported losses. If you have lost money to a scam, your funds are not sitting somewhere waiting to be recovered. In most cases, the money is already gone. Recovery agents who promise "We can recover your funds" are scammers looking to exploit you again. Never pay anyone to recover your lost money. Report the incident through the FBI IC3 complaint portal at ic3.gov.
FTC 2025: ~$3B lost to imposter and advance-fee scams.
