ROMANCE SCAM: I KNOW HE IS A SCAMMER. I STILL CAN'T LET GO. He didn't win your heart in a day. It took time. It took process. So there is no reason your feelings should disappear the moment you find out the truth. The truth changes what it was. It doesn't change what it felt like. Going back through the pictures? Re-reading the messages? You already know none of that helps. You do it anyway — because the absence feels worse than the reminder. That's the trap inside the trap. The scam continues in your nervous system long after the conversation ends. Here is what moving forward actually looks like: You don't block him on day one. You reduce. Gradually. Confronting him won't give you closure — it will give him another opening. Fill the space. That void is proof you are capable of deep connection. That capacity belongs to you. It was never his. FIELD EVIDENCE: "To die would be a happy ending." — Anonymous commenter. Zero financial loss. Critical emotional severity.

THE PROXY CALLER EXPLOIT The victim believed they were calling their bank. They were calling the operator. The number had been replaced — seamlessly — in the middle of a live conversation. The money transfer was authorised by the victim. The victim was the instrument, not the target. This is the first globally documented case of the Proxy Caller Exploit — confirmed by victim testimony submitted to OSB. No financial platform currently screens for this technique. It operates entirely within the victim's trust architecture. S-Pillar score: 5/5. Script sophistication at maximum. The fraud was invisible until the transfer cleared.

THE "UN DOCTOR" SCRIPT I've reviewed dozens of romance scam reports, and the "UN doctor in a conflict zone" story is one of the most common — and most financially damaging. In one documented case, a victim eventually wired over $50,000, with each payment tied to a new "UN processing fee," emergency, or clearance requirement. The payments were spaced out. Each request sounded reasonable. Each crisis felt urgent. That is how this script works. The scammer claims to be a surgeon or humanitarian working for the United Nations. Widowed or divorced. Moves fast emotionally — sends flowers, long messages, promises of a future. Introduces a child for sympathy. Creates a crisis — blocked bank account, urgent leave, travel approval. Asks you to email a "UN office" and pay processing fees. Reality: The United Nations does not charge the public for staff leave, travel, or admin processing. Official emails come only from "@un.org" domains. If money enters the relationship, it is not love — it is a romance scam.

ROMANCE SCAMS IN 2026: WHY PEOPLE STILL FALL VICTIM Loneliness and lack of awareness are the real roots of this problem. Who rejects love and affection in moments of emotional vulnerability? When attention and affection come when you are at your lowest, it's difficult to resist. Romance scammers don't mysteriously find their way into your heart — they penetrate during grief, loneliness, or major life changes, exploiting emotional vulnerability. Seniors remain one of the most vulnerable groups targeted. In the most recent FTC data, adults aged 60 and above lost approximately $277 million to romance scams alone. But the solution is not just awareness — it is human connection. Your little emotional check-ins may seem casual, but they go a long way in making your loved ones feel seen and cared for. When people feel loved and remembered by family, they are less likely to seek affection from strangers online. Technology can block scammers. Awareness can protect people. But human connection remains the strongest defence of all.

RECOVERY SCAMS: DON'T PAY TWICE In 2025, the U.S. Federal Trade Commission reported that imposter and advance-fee scams — a category that includes recovery scams — accounted for nearly $3 billion in reported losses. If you've lost money to a scam, your funds are not sitting somewhere waiting to be recovered. In most cases, the money is already gone — quickly withdrawn, transferred, or spent. Recovery "agents" who promise "We can recover your funds" are scammers looking to exploit you again. Never pay anyone to recover your lost money. If you are a victim, report the incident through the FBI's IC3 complaint portal. Fool me once, shame on you. Fool me twice, shame on me.

FROM LOVE TO LOSS: A SINGAPORE NURSE'S STORY A hardworking nurse in Singapore. After long shifts and isolation during COVID, she began chatting with a kind man online. He said he was an interior designer in Canada. They talked daily — sweet messages, photos, even dreams of marriage. Then he introduced her to "crypto trading." She tried it. The website looked real. Her money "grew." Over time she invested all her savings, took a loan, and sent over S$120,000 ($90,000 USD). Then it collapsed. The site demanded "withdrawal fees." When she refused, he vanished. Everything — the romance, the money, the platform — was fake. She was left with no money, huge debt, shame, heartbreak, and depression. She later filed for bankruptcy and started therapy. Now she shares her story to warn others. Don't invest because someone online says "trust me." Don't send money to someone you've never met. Trust your instincts — not smooth words. If this could happen to a smart, strong nurse — it can happen to anyone.

THE HIDDEN DANGERS OF ROMANCE SCAMS It's more than just losing money — the damage runs deep. EMOTIONAL & PSYCHOLOGICAL DAMAGE: Heartbreak and betrayal. Victims form real emotional bonds with scammers. Depression, anxiety, even PTSD are common. Many blame themselves, leading to isolation or suicidal thoughts. SEVERE FINANCIAL LOSS: Victims have lost thousands to millions. Some empty life savings, retirement funds, or business capital. Others take loans, remortgage homes, or max out credit cards. MIND CONTROL & MANIPULATION: Scammers use love bombing, fake emergencies, and guilt as weapons. Victims are isolated and taught to distrust family. In extreme cases, they unknowingly become money mules. IDENTITY THEFT & DATA BREACH: Scammers collect photos, IDs, bank info, and passwords. Some victims are blackmailed with private images — sextortion. LEGAL TROUBLE: Victims may unknowingly help transfer stolen money. Some face investigations, frozen accounts, or criminal charges. LONG-TERM RECOVERY IS HARD: Healing requires therapy, legal support, and financial rebuilding. Trust is broken, especially in future relationships. Shame on anyone who judges victims. Compassion is what's needed — not condemnation.

OIL RIG SCAMS: THE ROMANCE TRAP YOU DIDN'T SEE COMING Most people know about romance scams — but did you know there are over 20 types? You meet someone online — charming, professional, claims to be an engineer on an offshore oil rig. They say they're stationed in a remote location and can't come home yet. After love bombing, they tell you there's a crisis: "My equipment broke down, I need to transfer money urgently, but I can't access my account from here." They give you a fake bank login and ask you to transfer money for them. Suddenly the account gets "frozen." Then they beg you to cover the cost upfront — "I'll pay you back when I return!" There is NO oil rig. NO broken equipment. NO real bank account. Just an emotional setup to steal your money. Scammers use emotions as weapons. But now you know the trick. Remember this rule: Trust — but VERIFY.

WHAT IS AN ONLINE ROMANCE SCAM? Online romance scam is when an online love interest pretends to fall in love with you just to deceive you and steal your money. These scams typically occur on dating websites, social media platforms, or messaging apps, where the scammer creates a fake identity — often using stolen photos and fabricated personal details — to initiate and build a romantic relationship with you. Once they gain your trust, they start asking for money or financial and personal information. Whatever the excuse, they make it sound urgent or touching. And because you are a kind and caring person, you will be convinced to indulge the request. In most cases, romance scammers avoid phone calls or video calls. However, with the help of AI deepfake videos, some scammers can now stage fake video calls. Are you online dating and this sounds like your current situation? Trust, but verify.
